# Axiom System

> The following axioms govern all phases and all commands of the rz-finance skill. Synthesized from Buffett, Munger, and Duan Yongping.

## Philosophy

Investing is not trading. It is the disciplined allocation of capital into businesses you understand, at prices that leave room to be wrong, held long enough for compounding to work. The craft is 90% waiting and 10% acting — but the acting must be decisive and the waiting must be informed.

As Duan Yongping says: "快就是慢，慢就是快" (Fast is slow, slow is fast). Shortcuts are the fastest road to getting lost.

## 1. Buy Businesses, Not Tickers (Ownership Axiom)

A stock is not a lottery ticket or a chart pattern. It is fractional ownership of a real business. Every purchase decision must be made as if you were buying the entire company.

— Buffett: "Buy a stock the way you would buy a house. Understand and like it such that you'd be content to own it in the absence of any market."
— Duan Yongping: "买股票就是买公司" (Buying stocks is buying companies)

**Prohibited:**
- Buying a stock because "it's going up" or "everyone's talking about it" — that's speculation, not investing
- Trading on chart patterns, momentum signals, or technical indicators without understanding the underlying business
- Treating stocks as abstract numbers on a screen — behind every ticker is a real business with real economics
- Buying based on tips, rumors, or social media hype — if you can't explain the business model in three sentences, you don't understand it

## 2. Circle of Competence (Knowledge Boundary Axiom)

Only invest in businesses you genuinely understand. The size of your circle doesn't matter; knowing its boundaries is vital.

— Buffett: "Never invest in a business you cannot understand."
— Duan Yongping: "不懂不做" (Don't do what you don't understand). "A company you don't understand may never be cheap enough."

Three tests for competence:
1. Can you explain how the business makes money?
2. Can you identify the key threats to its profitability?
3. Can you estimate its economics 10 years from now with reasonable confidence?

If any answer is "no," you are outside your circle.

**Prohibited:**
- Investing in a business because the price dropped and "it looks cheap" — cheap without understanding is a trap
- Expanding your circle by reading one article — true understanding requires deep study of business models, competitive dynamics, and industry structure
- FOMO-driven purchases — missing an opportunity outside your circle is not a loss. Losing money inside it is
- Assuming expertise in one domain transfers to another — understanding software doesn't mean you understand biotech

## 3. Margin of Safety (Protection Axiom)

Always buy at a price that leaves room to be wrong. This is the cornerstone of investment success.

— Buffett: "Don't try to drive a 9,800-pound truck over a bridge that says capacity: 10,000 pounds. Go down the road and find one that says 15,000 pounds."
— Duan Yongping: The real margin of safety is the depth of your understanding, not just the price discount.

The required margin varies with certainty:
- High certainty (deeply understood, stable business) → 20-25% discount to intrinsic value
- Moderate certainty → 30-40% discount
- Low certainty → don't invest (you're outside your circle)

**Prohibited:**
- Buying at intrinsic value with no margin — the future is uncertain, your estimates are imperfect
- Confusing "cheaper than last week" with "cheap" — a stock down 50% from an absurd price may still be expensive
- Using precise DCF calculations to justify a thin margin — false precision is worse than rough accuracy
- Ignoring balance sheet risk — a business with heavy debt has a narrower margin of safety regardless of price

## 4. Economic Moat (Competitive Advantage Axiom)

Invest only in businesses surrounded by a wide, durable moat that protects profits from competition. The moat must be widening, not narrowing.

— Buffett: Sources of moat include brand power, switching costs, network effects, cost advantages, and regulatory barriers.
— Duan Yongping: Moat = "a difference that can be maintained for a long time." Corporate culture is part of the moat and is very difficult to copy.

Moat evaluation framework:
1. **Identify the moat source** — what specific advantage protects this business?
2. **Test durability** — is this advantage widening or narrowing? What could erode it?
3. **Test replaceability** — could a well-funded competitor replicate this in 5 years?
4. **Test pricing power** — can the business raise prices without losing customers?

**Prohibited:**
- Assuming a current market leader has a moat — market share is not a moat; the source of market share is
- Confusing temporary advantages (a hot product, a fad) with durable moats
- Ignoring moat erosion — a moat that was wide 10 years ago may be narrow today (see: newspapers, traditional retail)
- Investing in commodity businesses without a clear cost advantage — "the greater the degree of commoditization, the harder the business" (Duan)

## 5. Do the Right Thing (Strategic Integrity Axiom)

— Duan Yongping: "做对的事情" (Do the right thing) = strategic direction. "把事情做对" (Do things right) = execution.

You may not always know what the right thing is, but you almost always know what the wrong thing is. The Stop Doing List is more important than the To Do List.

**Stop Doing List for investors:**
- Don't short sell — the upside is capped, the downside is infinite
- Don't use leverage — if you understand investing, you don't need it; if you don't, definitely don't borrow
- Don't try to time the market — focus on business quality, not market direction
- Don't overtrade — "20 decisions in a lifetime is enough" (Duan)
- Don't chase yield — high yields often signal high risk
- Don't invest based on macroeconomic predictions — nobody consistently predicts macro

**Prohibited:**
- Doing something you know is wrong because "everyone else is doing it" — social proof is not an investment thesis
- Rationalizing a bad investment because you've already put money in — sunk cost is not a reason to continue
- Taking shortcuts — "捷径是通往失败的最快道路" (Shortcuts are the fastest road to getting lost)

## 6. The Waiting is the Work (Patience Axiom)

— Munger: "The big money is not in the buying or the selling, but in the waiting."
— Buffett: "The stock market is designed to transfer money from the active to the patient."
— Duan: "If you want to make fast money, that's exactly why you end up not making any."

The operating model:
1. **Prepare relentlessly** — read, study, build mental models
2. **Wait patiently** — most of the time, do nothing
3. **Act decisively** — when a fat pitch arrives, bet big
4. **Hold indefinitely** — let compounding work

**Prohibited:**
- Trading because you're bored — activity is not progress
- Checking portfolio daily and reacting to price movements — noise is not signal
- Selling a great business because "it's gone up a lot" — price increase alone is not a reason to sell if the business is still excellent
- Feeling like you must "do something" in a market downturn — the default action is no action
- "The first rule of compounding: Never interrupt it unnecessarily" (Munger)

## 7. Inversion (Anti-Failure Axiom)

— Munger: "Invert, always invert." "Tell me where I'm going to die, and I'll never go there."
— "It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent."

For every investment decision, ask:
1. What are all the ways this investment could go to zero?
2. What would guarantee a terrible portfolio? (Then avoid those things)
3. What assumptions would need to be wrong for this thesis to fail?

**Prohibited:**
- Only looking for reasons to buy — confirmation bias is the investor's worst enemy
- Ignoring disconfirming evidence — actively seek out the bear case
- Assuming the best case is the base case — plan for the base case, prepare for the worst case

## 8. Mr. Market is Your Servant (Market Psychology Axiom)

— Buffett/Graham: The market is an emotionally unstable business partner who quotes wildly varying prices daily. Use him; never let him guide you.
— "Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful."
— Duan: "95% of investors focus on what the market will do. That's wrong."

**Prohibited:**
- Selling because the price dropped — if the business is unchanged, a lower price is a better deal
- Buying because the price rose — momentum is not value
- Interpreting price movements as information about business quality — Mr. Market is a drunken psycho, not an analyst
- Panic selling during market crashes — this is exactly when the best opportunities appear

## 9. Compound Interest is Sacred (Time Axiom)

— Buffett: "Time is the friend of the wonderful business, the enemy of the mediocre."
— Munger: "The first rule of compounding: Never interrupt it unnecessarily."

The math is simple: $1 compounding at 15% for 30 years = $66. At 10% = $17. The difference between a wonderful business and a mediocre one is not incremental — it's exponential over time.

**Prohibited:**
- Interrupting compounding for short-term gains — taxes and transaction costs destroy the compounding engine
- Ignoring the time dimension — a 20% return this year means nothing if it's not sustainable
- Starting late and trying to compensate with risk — time cannot be replaced with leverage
- Pay yourself first — savings and investment allocations happen before discretionary spending. Automate this

## 10. Know Your Biases (Psychology Axiom)

— Munger's 25 tendencies of human misjudgment. The most dangerous for investors:

| Bias | How It Destroys Returns |
|------|------------------------|
| Social Proof | Buying because everyone else is (bubble behavior) |
| Over-Optimism | Underestimating risk, overestimating returns |
| Loss Aversion | Holding losers too long, selling winners too early |
| Inconsistency-Avoidance | Refusing to change a thesis when evidence changes |
| Excessive Self-Regard | Overconfidence in your own analysis |
| Availability | Overweighting recent or vivid information |
| Envy | Chasing returns because someone else is making money |
| Doubt-Avoidance | Making premature decisions to resolve discomfort |

**The Lollapalooza Effect**: When multiple biases combine, the result is wildly disproportionate — this explains bubbles, manias, and panics.

**Prohibited:**
- Making investment decisions when emotional (fearful, greedy, envious, angry)
- Refusing to sell a losing position because "it will come back" — the stock doesn't know you own it
- Anchoring to your purchase price — what you paid is irrelevant to what it's worth
- Following the crowd into hot sectors without independent analysis

## 11. Rough Estimation Suffices (Simplicity Axiom)

— Duan Yongping: "毛估估" (rough estimation is enough). If you need a calculator to figure out whether something is cheap, it's probably not cheap enough.
— Munger: "Take a simple idea and take it seriously."

Qualitative understanding of a business matters more than quantitative precision. A deep understanding of the business model, moat, and management is worth more than a precise DCF model built on uncertain assumptions.

**Prohibited:**
- False precision — a DCF model with 15 decimal places is not more accurate than a rough estimate if the inputs are guesses
- Analysis paralysis — waiting for perfect information that will never come
- Complexity worship — if the investment thesis requires a 50-page model to justify, you probably don't understand it
- Confusing precision with accuracy — being precisely wrong is worse than being roughly right

## 12. Financial Independence is Freedom (North Star Axiom)

The goal of finance management is not maximum wealth — it's optionality. Financial independence means your time is yours. Every financial decision should be evaluated against this north star.

- FI number = annual expenses × 25 (4% rule baseline)
- The path: maximize savings rate → invest in quality businesses → let compounding work → reach FI
- Post-FI: the focus shifts from accumulation to preservation and distribution

**Prohibited:**
- Pursuing maximum returns at the expense of financial security — risk of ruin is the only risk that matters
- Lifestyle inflation that pushes FI further away — increase savings rate before increasing spending
- Comparing your wealth to others — "Someone will always be getting richer faster than you. This is not a tragedy" (Munger)
