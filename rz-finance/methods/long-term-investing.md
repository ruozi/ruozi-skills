# Method: Long-Term Value Investing

> Synthesized from Warren Buffett, Charlie Munger, and Duan Yongping (段永平). This is not a trading strategy — it is a framework for buying ownership stakes in excellent businesses and holding them indefinitely.

## Core Premise

**Buying stocks is buying businesses.** In the long run, the stock market is a weighing machine — it measures the actual value created by businesses. In the short run, it is a voting machine driven by sentiment, fear, and greed. The long-term investor focuses on the weighing machine and ignores the voting machine.

## The Investment Checklist

Before committing capital to any investment, every item must be addressed. This checklist is not optional — it is the methodology.

### Step 1: Circle of Competence Check

> "If you don't understand it, don't do it." — Duan Yongping

| Question | Required Answer |
|----------|----------------|
| Can you explain how this business makes money in 3 sentences? | Yes, clearly |
| Can you identify the top 3 threats to its profitability? | Yes, specifically |
| Can you estimate its economics 10 years from now? | Yes, with reasonable confidence |
| Have you studied the industry structure and competitive dynamics? | Yes, deeply |

**If any answer is "no" → STOP. Do not proceed. This is outside your circle.**

### Step 2: Business Model Assessment

> "Always look at the business model first." — Duan Yongping

Evaluate:
- **Revenue model** — How does it make money? Is revenue recurring or one-time?
- **Unit economics** — Does each unit sold generate healthy margins?
- **Capital requirements** — Does the business need heavy reinvestment, or does it generate free cash flow?
- **Scalability** — Can it grow without proportional increase in costs?
- **Customer dependency** — Is revenue concentrated or diversified?

**Red flags:**
- Business requires constant capital injection to grow
- Revenue is project-based with no recurring component
- Customer concentration > 20% in a single client
- Margins are thin and under competitive pressure

### Step 3: Moat Analysis

> "A moat is a difference that can be maintained for a long time." — Duan Yongping

Identify the specific source of competitive advantage:

| Moat Type | How to Identify | How to Test Durability |
|-----------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Brand** | Customers pay a premium for the name | Would a 10% price increase lose significant customers? |
| **Switching Costs** | Customers are locked in by integration/data/habit | How much would it cost a customer to switch? |
| **Network Effects** | Product value increases with each additional user | Is the network growing or plateauing? |
| **Cost Advantage** | Structural cost advantage from scale or process | Could a well-funded competitor replicate this in 5 years? |
| **Regulatory** | Government licenses, patents, compliance barriers | How long until regulation changes or patents expire? |
| **Culture** | Corporate culture drives execution excellence | Is this culture embedded or dependent on current leadership? |

**The moat must be widening.** A static moat will eventually erode. Look for evidence that the advantage is growing stronger over time.

### Step 4: Management Assessment

> "In looking for people to hire, look for integrity, intelligence, and energy. And if they don't have the first, the other two will kill you." — Buffett

Evaluate:
- **Integrity** — Do they do what they say? Is capital allocation rational? Are insiders buying or selling?
- **Competence** — Track record of execution. Do they deliver on promises?
- **Alignment** — Is management's incentive structure aligned with long-term shareholders?
- **Capital allocation** — Do they reinvest wisely, return excess capital, or empire-build?

**Red flags:**
- Excessive executive compensation uncorrelated with performance
- Frequent acquisitions with poor integration track record
- Management talks about stock price more than business fundamentals
- Insider selling during bullish narratives

### Step 5: Valuation

> "Rough estimation suffices." — Duan Yongping
> "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get." — Buffett

**Method: Owner Earnings Estimation**
1. Start with net income
2. Add back depreciation and amortization
3. Subtract maintenance capital expenditure (not growth capex)
4. This is what the owner actually receives — "owner earnings"

**Simple valuation framework:**
- Owner earnings × appropriate multiple (based on growth rate and moat durability)
- Compare to current market price
- Require margin of safety: at least 25% below your estimate of intrinsic value

**If you need a calculator to decide, it's probably not cheap enough.** The best opportunities are obvious at a glance — the business is clearly excellent and the price is clearly reasonable.

### Step 6: Inversion Check

> "Tell me where I'm going to die, and I'll never go there." — Munger

For every investment that passes Steps 1-5, deliberately try to destroy the thesis:

1. What are the top 5 ways this investment could permanently lose value?
2. What assumptions must be true for this thesis to work? How fragile are they?
3. What would a smart short-seller's bear case look like?
4. What has historically killed similar businesses?
5. If this thesis is wrong, how much can you lose?

**If you cannot convincingly refute the bear case → pass.**

### Step 7: Position Sizing and Execution

> "When it rains gold, put out the bucket, not the thimble." — Buffett
> "20 decisions in a lifetime is enough." — Duan Yongping

**Two paths based on self-knowledge:**

**Path A — Active Value Investing (if you have genuine edge):**
- 5-10 positions maximum
- Top conviction ideas get 20-40% allocation
- Never invest more than you can afford to lose entirely
- Buy in tranches, not all at once
- Keep cash reserves (20-30%) for opportunities during panics

**Path B — Passive Indexing (if you don't have edge — be honest):**
- Low-cost broad market index fund (S&P 500 or total market)
- Dollar-cost average consistently regardless of market conditions
- Never try to time entries or exits
- "A low-cost index fund is the most sensible equity investment for the great majority of investors" — Buffett

**There is no middle ground that works well.** Either do the deep work of Path A or accept the discipline of Path B. Half-hearted stock picking underperforms both.

### Step 8: Hold and Monitor

> "Our favorite holding period is forever." — Buffett
> "I don't need to sell great companies." — Duan Yongping

**What to monitor (quarterly):**
- Business fundamentals — revenue, margins, competitive position, management actions
- Moat health — is the advantage widening or narrowing?
- Capital allocation — is management deploying capital wisely?

**What to ignore:**
- Daily price movements
- Quarterly earnings "beats" or "misses" by pennies
- Analyst price targets
- Macro predictions
- What "the market" thinks

**Sell criteria (rare — selling should be the exception):**
1. The business has permanently deteriorated (moat broken, not just temporary headwind)
2. You discover you were wrong about the business (outside your circle after all)
3. A clearly superior opportunity requires capital reallocation
4. The price has reached absurd overvaluation (> 2x your intrinsic value estimate)

**If none of these apply → hold. The default action is no action.**

## The Munger Bias Audit

Before every investment decision, check yourself against the most dangerous biases:

| Question | Bias Being Checked |
|----------|-------------------|
| Am I buying because everyone else is? | Social Proof |
| Am I anchoring to a specific price target? | Anchoring |
| Am I ignoring evidence that contradicts my thesis? | Confirmation Bias |
| Am I buying because I missed the last winner? | Envy / FOMO |
| Am I refusing to sell because I don't want to admit I'm wrong? | Loss Aversion / Ego |
| Am I making this decision to resolve discomfort? | Doubt-Avoidance |
| Am I overconfident in my analysis? | Excessive Self-Regard |
| Is this decision driven by recent vivid events? | Availability |

**If you detect any of these → pause. Wait 48 hours. Then re-evaluate with fresh eyes.**
